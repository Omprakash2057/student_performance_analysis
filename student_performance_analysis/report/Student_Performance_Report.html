01 · Executive Summary

What the Data Tells Us

This analysis examined 10,000 student records spanning Grades 9–12 to understand the relationship between behavioural patterns (attendance, study time) and academic performance (subject scores and overall GPA-equivalent score). Students were segmented into three performance tiers — High, Medium, and Low — based on their overall score distribution.

The findings are unambiguous: attendance and daily study hours are the two strongest predictors of academic performance. High-performing students attend class 32 percentage points more frequently and study more than twice as many hours per day compared to their low-performing peers. These behavioural gaps translate directly into a 35-point score differential across all subjects.

02 · Subject Performance

Score Breakdown Across Subjects

Performance gaps are consistent across all four subjects — Math, Science, English, and History — confirming that underperformance is systemic rather than subject-specific. Science shows the largest absolute gap (34.9 pts) between High and Low tiers; English the smallest (34.3 pts), suggesting a slightly more even distribution of language ability.

05 · Study Hours

Study Time Distribution

Study hours cluster tightly around each tier's mean. High performers dominate the 5–8 hour range, while Low performers cluster in the 1–3 hour window. There is minimal overlap beyond the 4-hour mark, implying a practical threshold: students who study fewer than 3 hours per day are unlikely to achieve top-tier outcomes regardless of other factors.

07 · Recommendations

Suggested Actions

  • 📋
    Implement early attendance alerts. Flag students whose attendance drops below 70% in any 4-week window. Data shows this threshold is strongly associated with sliding into the Low tier. Automated weekly reports to tutors would enable early intervention.
  • 📚
    Introduce structured after-school study programmes. The gap between Low (2.1 hrs) and High (6.5 hrs) daily study suggests unequal access to study environments. Supervised evening sessions targeting Medium-tier students could shift a meaningful portion towards High-tier outcomes.
  • 🎯
    Focus resources on the Medium tier. With 50% of students (5,040) sitting in the Medium tier, targeted support here offers the highest return. Even a 10-point average score uplift across this group would significantly improve institution-wide performance metrics.
  • 📊
    Track subject-specific interventions separately. While overall patterns are consistent, subject-level tracking would reveal whether targeted tutoring in Science (highest tier gap) yields faster improvements than English (smallest tier gap).
08 · Methodology

How This Was Done

Data Collection 10,000 synthetic student records modelled on real-world grade distributions across 4 subjects and 4 grade levels (9–12)
Cleaning & Grouping Pandas for data wrangling; SQL-style groupby aggregations; outlier clipping applied to attendance and study hours
Visualisation Matplotlib + Seaborn for 4 chart types: grouped bar, line trend, distribution bar, and horizontal KPI comparison
